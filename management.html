Teacher Actions / Strategies

Theoretical Frameworks / Models, Legislative Requirements and Systems Policies

TIER 1
Ethical, respectful, unobtrusive, non-punitive strategies for managing the classroom and responding to low-level undesired behaviours

Setting a class routine and having clear expectations – routines and expectations must be established from the beginning.

Positive reinforcement – praise and rewards the encourage all students to participate in desired behaviour. E.g. commend student 1 on their efforts into their advanced project.

Ensure learning is engaging and relevant for all students. E.g. provide visual aids and clear instructions for student 3 to help them stay on task.

Give respectful and gentle reminders of classroom expectations and rules using non-disruptive approaches.

These tier 1 intervention strategies are supported by the PBS framework highlighting the importance of a structures and engaging classroom environment. By setting clear expectations and routines provides predictability and reduces uncertainty in the classroom, preventing undesired behaviour. Positive reinforcement encourages desired behaviours whilst supporting the overall engagement of all students. Ensuring students stay focused and engaged, by providing visual aids, aligns with PBS’s aim of fostering supportive learning environments. Gentle, non-disruptive reminders of classroom rules and expectations help to manage behaviour in and unobtrusive way, ultimately contributing to the positive learning environment. In the context of Dreikurs’ Goal Theory, students’ basic needs for attention, power, revenge and escape can be addressed by tier 1 strategies. By establishing a structure learning environment with clear rules and expectations helps in preventing undesired behaviours driven by power or attention seeking. Students needs for attention can be met positively by positive reinforcement, reducing the need for them to “act out” to gain attention. To help reduce students fear of failure, providing additional aids and giving engaging instruction allows the tasks to be more accessible for students, addressing escape behaviours.

TIER 2
Ethical and respectful strategies for responding to persistent undesired behaviours within the classroom

Provide student 1 with advanced tasks requiring increased intellectual stimulation to keep them engaged. E.g. extension activities, complex problem-solving tasks. This student should be encouraged to participate in enrichment programs to increase their motivation and decrease disengagement and disruptive behaviour. Schedule meetings to discuss the student’s interest and progress so they feel valued and heard.

Student 2 should have a structured break routine, where they can have planned, short breaks to regain energy and focus on the class work. Visual schedules or timers can be used to manage this routine. Create a behavioural contract with the student and a higher authority to set goals for specific behaviours and progress in class, with a reward system. This will help them to keep their focus and have something to work towards. Physical cues are important to help the student to stay focused, e.g. colour cards to show when it’s time to change activities.

Student 3 should participate in guided social skills training to help navigate interactions with their peers more successfully. E.g. scenarios and practicing appropriate responses. The use of quiet spaces and sensory tools will help this student manage any sensory overloads that may occur which will help them maintain their focus. Clear communication and visual aids will help this student to better understand transitions and expectations. Visual timetables can be used for them to anticipate and get ready for any changes in their routine.

PBS aims to address the learning needs of student with learning difficulties. Incorporating structured rest breaks into students’ routines helps to keep students on task and maintain focus. Behavioural contracts written collaboratively by the student and teacher ensure the student understands the in their own words the expectations of them. Setting these goals for a student will help them to maintain their focus. Challenging activities help better engage students preventing disruptive behaviour stemming from boredom. In the context pf Dreikurs’ Goal Theory, structured breaks in the student’s routine allows the student to move around removing the need for power arising from being forced to sit still for long periods at a time, reducing undesired behaviour from occurring. Students’ senses of empowerment is increased when developing behaviour contracts with their teachers, as they feel like they have a sense of control over their expectations and the rewards they have the opportunity to obtain, addressing their need for power in a positive and constructive way. Giving students the opportunity to excel and expand their knowledge and skills recognises their need for attention reducing the need for them to participate in disruptive behaviour to gain that need. Escape behaviours can be reduced by providing students with sensory accommodations avoiding any overwhelming sensory inputs. These accommodations can help students to be more focus and feel a sense of safety when they are in the classroom, fostering a learning environment that is both positive and inclusive.

TIER 3
Practical strategies for managing challenging behaviour within the classroom. Strategies for communicating sensitively and confidentially with parents/carers, and engaging with them throughout the process. The role of, and strategies for working with, professional colleagues, specialist teachers and/or school community liaison personnel to support Emergency responses.

With student 1, collaboratively create a personalised learning plan that includes both challenging tasks and self-directed learning to ensure that they are being adequately challenging. E.g. expert in a field of interest. This student should be enrolled in enrichment programs and extra-curricular activities that they are interest in. This student’s engagement will increase and as they are participating in activities that are relevant for them. Meeting with a school counsellor can help this student to discuss why they are feeling bored and disengaged and can work together to create preventative strategies for these feelings.

Student 2 is provided with a support teacher to help them maintain their focus on the content and help them better understand what is in front of them. The individualised attention will help prevent this student from participating in undesired behaviours. Ensure the learning environment has flexibility, allowing this student to have some movement during their learning, e.g. standing desks, wiggle seats, to accommodate for their need for physical activity to help ten regain their focus.

Include sensory tools and strategies that are easily accessible in student 3’s learning to help manage any sensory overload, e.g. quiet space, headphones, fidget tools. Incorporate social strategies to help the student navigate socialising with their peers and other teaching staff. Teach social skills and appropriate behaviour, with specialised instruction techniques. Work with specialists to create a behaviour intervention plan (BIP) tailored to this student.

Regularly communicate with students’ parents/carers regarding their progress. Use parents / carers preferred method of contact to ensure comfort.

Ensure confidentiality when discussing students, record information ensure it is only accessed by authorised staff / educational professionals.

Discuss students’ strengths before discussing any areas that are of concern.

Actively listen to what the students’ parents / carers are saying.

Use a team approach and work together with parents / carers.

PBS has a main focus on proactive, individualised and supportive strategies to manage quite challenging, repetitive behaviour. It highlight the importance of tailoring interventions relent for the needs of different learning needs for all students. Fostering supportive and structed learning environments will enhance the engagement of all students by addressing the root cause of the behaviour. In the context of Dreikurs’ Goal Theory these tier 3 intervention strategies provide students with the desired feelings on attention power and escape, by providing structure and intellectual challenge. By incorporating these strategies into the classroom these students will feel supported, reducing the opportunity to participate in disruptive behaviour.

PBS supports these intervention strategies by highlighting the importance for both the school community and parents/carers in being consistent and transparent when communicating with one another. Seeking parents/carers input promotes collaborative behaviour management to support the student’s success in all aspects of their lives. Educators can better understand the needs of students by using empathetic language to address what their behaviour is driven by, supporting Dreikurs’ Goal Theory. Highlighting the strengths of students focuses on encouraging desired behaviour through a mutual understanding and respect.
